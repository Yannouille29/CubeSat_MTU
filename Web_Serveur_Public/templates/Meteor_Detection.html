Introduction :

A final improvement could be added as part of the CubeSat project. To help enhance the project, it is the detection of meteor that was chosen. The goal of this improvement was to detect the meteors with a SDR. To add his improvement, the first step was to understand the general operation of the meteor detection.

Understanding the operation of :

The principle is the following one : a transmitter sends out radio waves toward the sky at an altitude of about 100 km. When a meteor enters the Earth's atmosphere, it begins to burn up briefly creating atmospheric ionized gases. These atmospheric ionized gases create conditions which are reflective to terrestrial radio waves. This occurs at an altitude of about 80 to 100 kilometers. The emitted radio wave can then be temporarily reflected by the ionized gases of the meteor trail, back to Earth. The signal can then be trapped by a receiver whose frequency is matched to the one of the transmitter. The trapped signal is called a meteor echo.

Transmitter Station Selection :

The summary is as follows: To integrate meteor detection into the CubeSat project, it was crucial to find a suitable transmitting station located near the Nimbus Center, where the receiving antenna was situated. The distance between the transmitter and the receiver determined the type of antenna to use. If the distance was less than 200 km, a simple wire dipole antenna was sufficient. Otherwise, a new antenna configuration was needed. The Beacon Stations website in Ireland was valuable for locating the nearest transmitting station, which was the EI2DKH station tuned to 144.178 MHz and located 84.52 km from the Nimbus Center. This distance was critical for the project.

Hardware :

We chose to use an RTL-SDR, a receiver capable of capturing signals in a range from 24 MHz to 1850 MHz. This decision proved advantageous as the RTL-SDR was already in our possession and used with our ground station, thereby avoiding supply problems, which was crucial given the very limited time available.

We built the antenna using available materials, deviating from the original diagram's specifications. The materials used included a 50-ohm coaxial cable, copper wire for the horizontal parts, electrical tape instead of insulators, a BNC cable with crocodile clips, and connectors to link the BNC cable to the coaxial cable and connect the other end of the coaxial cable to the SDR.

Software :

Regarding the software for controlling and receiving data from the SDR, we opted for SDR Console, a choice recommended by many websites. Available sources provided instructions on how to use it, simplifying the setup of our reception system.

This software had two main windows : a window at the top showing the spectrum of the signal with frequency as the horizontal scale and amplitude as the vertical scale and one window at the bottom with a waterfall display.

Using the waterfall display is the easiest way to observe meteor echoes. It is important to note that Meteors could have the size of a grain of sand. If the meteors detected were big, the strength of their echoes was represented by their brightness

Place for the antenna :

After checking technical specifications, building an antenna for 144.178 MHz became essential due to the lack of one at Nimbus Center. Challenges included dimensions, positioning, and interference concerns. The antenna had to be placed in a location free from interference and linked to a computer with software. The courtyard between Nimbus and Rubicon Centers offered a suitable space.

But the most suitable option would still be an open area, not surrounded by buildings containing potential sources of interference, such as the Nimbus. (Within the given time frame, it was the simplest solution).

Dimensioning of the antenna :

Formulas allow for the calculation of antenna dimensions based on the frequency of the transmitting station. The appropriate height and wavelength of the antenna can be determined based on the transmitting station's frequency.

Calculations for 144.178 MHz resulted in a half-wavelength of 0.99 meters for the antenna, equivalent to approximately 0.49 meters for a quarter-wavelength. The height above the ground was approximately 1.1 meters.

Following modifications and real-world testing, the copper wires measured 0.85 meters each, and the antenna above the ground measured around 0.96 meters. The discrepancy between theory and experiment was attributed to interference, as well as the added length of the copper wires due to the BNC cable.

Real trial and result :

During the trials, the outdoor antenna remained unchanged, but the coaxial cable connected to the spectrum analyzer for the test, was connected to the SDR (Software-Defined Radio). The SDR was then linked to a computer equipped with SDR Console software and set to the frequency of 144.178 MHz, corresponding to the transmitter's frequency. A signal was identified at this frequency on the software, allowing for centering it by clicking on it. It was also possible to adjust the bandwidth based on the signal's strength.

The software window provided the capability to observe frequency changes. However, due to a lack of time, the nature of these frequency changes could not be determined. It is likely that some of them were due to aircraft, given the proximity to Cork Airport.
