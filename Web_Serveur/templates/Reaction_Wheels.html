Introduction :

A CubeSat is a small, cube-shaped satellite widely used for cost-effective space missions. We briefly described how it operates and what it can be used for on the homepage.

As part of this project at Nimbus, it was necessary to make improvements to the basic support structure of the CubeSat. In this context, the addition of reaction wheels to a CubeSat represents an intriguing technological advancement, as it enables precise and efficient control of the satellite's orientation. In this article, we will explore the possibility of integrating two reaction wheels into a CubeSat and how they could enhance its capabilities.

Study of the choice :

Commencing our deliberation on improving the control of our CubeSat's orientation, several options were available to us, and we will describe them below.

Gyroscopes : The gyroscopes used in satellites are sophisticated mechanical devices that utilize the principle of angular momentum conservation to determine and maintain the satellite's spatial orientation. They consist of a rapidly rotating rotor whose axis of rotation remains fixed relative to space. When the satellite undergoes changes in orientation, the gyroscope responds by preserving its own orientation, allowing sensors to measure angular variations.

Advantage : A key advantage of CMGs (Control Moment Gyroscope) over Reaction Wheels lies in their capability to produce higher torques owing to their greater angular momentum capacity. This equips them with superior capabilities to effectively manage substantial disturbances resulting from external forces. Furthermore, CMGs are not plagued by concerns such as wheel saturation, which is a common occurrence in Reaction Wheels under high-output conditions.

Default :Nevertheless, CMGs also have certain drawbacks. They are relatively more complex systems requiring precise maintenance for optimal performance. Furthermore, implementing multiple CMGs for redundancy can significantly increase the spacecraft's weight and cost.

Reaction Wheels : A "reaction wheel" system in a satellite is primarily composed of a rotating wheel mounted on an axis, an electric motor to control its speed, sensors to measure the satellite's orientation, and control electronics. When the satellite needs to change its orientation, the electric motor spins the wheel at a specific speed, generating angular momentum to make precise orientation adjustments. This enables the satellite to maintain a stable orientation and adapt to necessary changes to accomplish its space mission.

Advantage : On the flip side, Reaction Wheels are comparatively simpler and more cost-effective to produce and upkeep. They also deliver immediate response times, rendering them well-suited for small satellites or situations demanding real-time adjustments. Additionally, Reaction Wheels excel in precision thanks to their capacity to generate smaller torque increments. They require less power consumption than CMGs as they do not need continuous power supply once they achieve their desired rotational speed.

Default : However, Reaction Wheels, despite their benefits, do face certain constraints. One of these is their lower potential for angular momentum compared to CMGs. This limitation hinders their ability to effectively manage significant disturbances originating from external sources. Additionally, Reaction Wheels can encounter saturation problems in situations demanding high torque, necessitating specific programming or intricate control algorithms for resolution.

We have made the final decision to opt for reaction wheels because their installation is simpler and requires less specialized equipment to conduct initial tests within just two months. Additionally, the potential impact of a new component order on the outcome is a major concern given the time constraints. Furthermore, it is easier to find numerous examples and sources of information available on reaction wheels and their setup, which will facilitate our development and testing process. Moreover, for our use case, a less power-hungry technology in terms of power supply suits us better, even though it is slightly more cumbersome.

Understanding Reaction Wheels :

Reaction wheels are devices used on Earth in projects involving the balance of objects, but they are primarily employed in satellites and other spacecraft to control the orientation of the object. The basic principle involves changing the rotation speed of one or more wheels placed at strategic points on the spacecraft to achieve a desired rotation like we describe in the first part.

We didn't emphasize it, but the goal is to set up an experimental prototype that allows a real visualization of the impact of reaction wheels on the behavior of a CubeSat.

Integration of Reaction Wheels into a CubeSat :

The mechanical design: So, we have chosen to integrate a total of 2 reaction wheels. This way, we have the ability to rotate on all 3 axes, even though it requires a sequencing that would not have been necessary with 3 wheels. (Indeed, to reach certain positions, we will need to activate the X-axis wheel separately from the Y-axis wheel). Therefore, we have already started to think about how to optimize the integration of the wheels into the 10cm x 10cm CubeSat. This visualization was necessary and possible through the use of SolidWorks software, which we will subsequently use to 3D-print the various support pieces.

The first two images we see are the initial versions, and the video clearly shows the final rendering with rotations on the X-axis and Z-axis at the end, in cases where the wheels turn one after the other.

Control Systems: In our basic CubeSat, we utilize an Arduino SparkFun Pro Micro board, which comes equipped with both analog and digital pins. To directly control the motor speed, all we need to do is add a "speed control" device like an "ESC_Speed_Control" and utilize one of the pins to send a PWM signal from the SparkFun. Therefore, our setup would consist of 2 DC motors, 2 speed controllers, an Arduino SparkFun board, a battery, and our gyroscope sensor.

If we wish to integrate our software to automatically orient our CubeSat for taking pictures of the Earth, we can achieve this by programming Arduino on the SparkFun board and implementing a PID controller.

Software: In our case, we have two possible software solutions to integrate into our CubeSat. The first solution would enable automatic orientation as soon as the CubeSat is ejected from the rocket, ensuring full autonomy from launch to mission completion. The second possibility would be to implement a module for receiving data, allowing us to control the orientation from the ground station. This option would be much more complex to implement if we want to adhere to real mission constraints.

Power Supply: In our case, the reaction wheels will be powered by a battery, which is autonomous because it is connected to all the solar panels.

Our prototype :

So we designed our prototype after 3D printing the necessary parts. For this, as previously mentioned, we used the SparkFun Arduino. However, the idea of using the ESC speed controller was changed as we didn't have it in our possession. So initially, we used the DRV8838, which allows exactly the same functionality but only with a single motor. However, using two, we eventually opted to use the L293D.

It allows controlling the two directions for both motors. However, a problem arose: it doesn't allow PWM control as easily. For our initial prototype, we decided that this could be improved in the future, giving us a chance to conduct tests with the remaining time. (After thorough research, we finally understood how to integrate PWM with the enable pins.)

The first video shows the initial tests with the DRV8838 on one motor, while the photo depicts the L293D that we are currently using along with its internal schematic.

During the last week, we had just enough time to develop a prototype of our Reaction Wheel control board on a veroboard. This way, we have a fairly lightweight and discreet prototype that we will integrate into a casing.

Since we didn’t have an on/off button, we placed a jumper to close the circuit and power it up. All of this is powered by a 7.2V motor battery and stepped down to 5V for the SparkFun Pro Micro.

PCB Creation on Kicad :

We also took the time to create a PCB with our initial version of functional code (we did not implement changes in the case where we introduce PWM with the L239D in this version).

Demonstrate proper functioning :

In our case, we aim to set up a robust demonstration to observe the effects of implementing 2 reaction wheels in our "CubeSat" format. There are three different ways to observe the operation.

1. The first method involves suspension using wires attached to all four sides of a single face of the cube. This approach is the simplest to implement and the least costly.

2. The second option is a simulation system using 3D printing or simple materials, which entails nesting three circles or squares to replicate free movement.

3. The final method is an air-bearing system, which suspends a sphere containing the CubeSat in a "levitating" fashion through air propulsion from a support. This allows for 360-degree freedom of rotation in all directions. However, the latter method appears to be somewhat complex to set up.

Final prototype :

We have thus managed to set up a functional but improvable final prototype. The video below demonstrates the prototype using the code that does not yet employ PWM (Pulse Width Modulation). However, a specific Arduino code has been placed on GitHub, enhancing the prototype's functionality in case we gain access to PWM.

Conclusion :

Regarding our project, we have completed a functional version that includes a visualization of the effects of using reaction wheels. Naturally, the project is intended to be improved in several ways. This includes the development of the PCB and its cleaner integration into the overall structure, optimizing precision, implementing a system with passive components to deliver enough current for motor start-up peaks, thus avoiding constant operation at 7 volts.

Additionally, we are considering creating a 2-unit CubeSat, where one unit would be traditional, and the second would comprise reaction wheels. This configuration is entirely feasible and relatively straightforward since the SparkFun code is similar in some aspects, except for motor control changes, and there is available space on the sensor board.
